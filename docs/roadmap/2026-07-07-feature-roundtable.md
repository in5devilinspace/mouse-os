Cast: 🕵️ Mary (Business Analyst) · 📋 John (Product Manager) · 🏗️ Winston (System Architect) · 🎨 Sally (UX Designer). Orchestrated by Fable in subagent mode — one real agent behind each persona, two rounds, shared room.

## Round 1 — independent takes

🕵️ **Mary:** The evidence points one place for me: Talon owns the power-user who'll memorize a command alphabet, and Dragon doesn't even exist on Linux — so our wedge is the non-technical user on Ubuntu who wants a conversation, not a grammar, and the talk-back cursor is exactly that wedge. That's the one I'd fight for: when a command fails silently, a hands-free user has no recovery path, but a cursor that says "I found two Firefox windows — which one?" turns every error into a dialogue, and even robotic spd-say delivers that today. The one I'd defer is the Reolink camera viewer — it's the founder scratching his own itch, it doesn't serve the segment, and a v1 workaround is just saying "point to Chrome, click" on a browser tab showing the camera feed. Ship the voice-to-cursor core with talk-back confirmation, and we have something Talon structurally can't copy without rethinking their whole silent-command model.

📋 **John:** Here's my take: the smallest real win after today's v1 is the AppIndicator with a hard mute/wake toggle and a "did it hear me" status light — a hands-free user who can't see or control whether the mic is live doesn't trust the system, and trust is the whole product. I'd defer the Reolink camera viewer without hesitation; it's a personal itch, not an assistive-shell feature, and frankly RTSP-in-a-browser-tab solves it today with zero code from us. The one I'll fight for is the talking cursor — not because speech-to-speech is easy, but because spd-say confirmations ("clicked Firefox") are cheap right now and they close the feedback loop that makes voice control usable on Wayland where we can't always guarantee the click landed. Speak-back confirmations first, persona later, cameras never — that's my sequencing.

🏗️ **Winston:** The architectural risk here is that we're really looking at three different products wearing one trench coat — an input stack, a conversational agent, and a media viewer — and the keystone is the boring one: the uinput daemon with a clean intent bus in front of it, because once "voice becomes a synthetic input event" is a solid contract, glide, speed profiles, eye-tracking, even the web agent are just new publishers on the same bus. I'd defer the speech-to-speech talking cursor: full-duplex audio means barge-in and echo cancellation while Vosk is trying to listen, which is a swamp — ship one-way spd-say confirmations and nobody loses the experience. The one I'd fight for is the GNOME Shell extension for window-by-name lookup, and early, because without it "point to Firefox" degrades to percent-coordinate guessing and the whole product demos like a toy. And the Reolink cameras? That's an RTSP viewer — fine as a launcher target, but it should never live inside the shell's architecture.

🎨 **Sally:** The founder's instinct that the cursor should talk back is actually the right one — when your voice is your whole body on this machine, silence after a command is terrifying, because you don't know if it misheard you or just froze. But I'd defer the full speech-to-speech *persona*: with spd-say sounding like a 1998 robot, a chatty companion becomes noise you have to talk over all day, and what the user actually needs is short, quiet confirmations — "clicked," "didn't catch that" — not conversation. The list completely misses error recovery, and that's the one I'll fight for: a single always-hot "stop / undo / never mind" that works instantly, because every misrecognition this person has to fix costs them more speech, and speech is a muscle that fatigues by 2pm just like a wrist does. Get trust and recovery right in v1 and the cameras, the web agent, all of it can wait — nobody adopts a hands-free shell they had to rescue with a caregiver's mouse twice in the first hour.

## Round 2 — the clash

🕵️ **Mary:** On the wedge — it survives, because the wedge was never voice quality, it was the dialogue loop: Talon's entire model is silent one-way commands, so even a robot voice that answers back is structurally differentiated, and the panel's disjoint-lexicon design proves the loop works today — but a wedge is two-way by definition, so what can't wait a year isn't the ElevenLabs persona, it's the disambiguation half, "which Firefox?", which needs Winston's extension and a tiny yes/no follow-up grammar, not speech-to-speech. On cameras, the evidence cuts both ways: products do die when they lose their first devoted user, but they also die building a media viewer for an n of one — the founder's actual job is "check my cameras hands-free," and one grammar entry that launches his RTSP browser tab serves that job for a day's work, so he keeps his cameras and we keep our scope; his "complete operating system" and that hype landing page are an expectations problem we correct in writing now, before user zero becomes churn zero. One caveat: I'm assuming Sally's always-hot stop-and-undo made it into the panel's sixty utterances — if it didn't, it leapfrogs everything.

📋 **John:** Winston, I'll concede half and keep half: the panel's grid+nudge decision proves the daemon works without your extension, which means the extension is now a delight feature, not a keystone — but the landing page just publicly promised "v1.1 desktop integration," so your extension jumps to my v1.1 list on marketing-commitment grounds alone, while the tray stays first because a paralyzed user who can't verify the mic is dead won't keep the product installed long enough to ever say "point to Firefox." On cameras — fine, "never" was me being cute, and I don't get to overrule my only user; but I'm converting his ask into one grammar utterance, "show my cameras," that launches the RTSP tab full-screen — he gets his four Reolinks by voice this week, we write zero camera code, and the shell stays a shell. And honestly I want "stop / undo / never mind" carved into the v1 sixty-utterance grammar before we ship tonight, because error recovery isn't a feature, it's the floor.

🏗️ **Winston:** I'll concede half of it — the disjoint lexicon is genuinely clever, but the mic-pause is the landmine: the moment the system is talking is exactly the moment the user most needs Sally's "stop," and a deaf-while-speaking shell will eat that word from every user who instinctively talks over a robot. Ship v1 with the pause if you must, but understand it's a stopgap — and note the fix is already in their own design, because a disjoint lexicon means the mic can stay hot on a tiny three-word interrupt grammar during TTS with no echo cancellation at all. On ordering, John wins and I'll re-rank rather than fight: a tray with hard mute is two days of work against my extension's two weeks — but the extension stays in v1.1, because grid coordinates make every pixel *reachable* while window-by-name makes it a *conversation*, which is Mary's entire wedge. And to our founder, with respect: your four Reolinks get a launcher entry in one afternoon and never a line of code inside the shell, and speech-to-speech stays parked — the landing page can promise v2 whatever it likes, but we don't drain swamps to keep a hype page honest; we ship the boring bus and let it earn the roadmap.

🎨 **Sally:** Mic-pause is an accessibility bug wearing a fix's clothes — the one moment a user most needs "stop" is while the machine is talking about the wrong thing it just did — but I'll accept it for today's ship only under a hard contract: every utterance capped at two words, roughly 600 milliseconds of audio, mic hot again within 200 milliseconds of TTS ending, and a visible state light that shows deaf-versus-listening so nobody is screaming "undo" into a dead microphone without knowing it. Anything longer than a second of speech — Mary's lovely "I found two Firefox windows, which one?" included — must not ship until it's interruptible, or we've built a robot that lectures paralyzed people who can't hang up on it. On cameras: founder-as-user-zero is a real signal about the person, not the product — he gets his itch scratched today with a voice bookmark, "open my cameras," one grammar entry and zero code inside the shell, and that's my final answer even to the man signing the checks. And someone please tell whoever wrote "v2 hands-free OS" on that landing page that our first users will find it from a hospital bed — we do not get to overpromise to them.

## Outcome — prioritized roadmap (consensus + dissents)

**Amendments to v1 (tonight's build):**
1. `stop` / `undo` / `never mind` join the v1 grammar as always-hot interrupts (John: "error recovery isn't a feature, it's the floor").
2. Sally's TTS contract: feedback utterances ≤2 words wherever possible; mic re-arms ≤200 ms after TTS ends; listening/deaf state always visible (CLI status line until the tray exists). Long disambiguation sentences do not ship until interruptible.
3. `show my cameras` / `open my cameras` = one grammar utterance that launches the founder's Reolink RTSP view (browser tab or ffplay wall). Zero camera code inside the shell.

**v1.1 (ranked, room consensus):**
1. Always-hot interrupt channel during TTS — keep the mic hot on a tiny interrupt-only grammar while speaking (Winston's fix, replaces mic-pause).
2. AppIndicator tray: hard mute/wake toggle, "heard you" status light, settings entry.
3. GNOME Shell extension for window-by-name lookup + yes/no follow-up grammar → "point to Firefox → which of the two?" disambiguation dialogue (Mary's wedge).

**v2 (parked, explicit):** speech-to-speech persona (ElevenLabs-style) — parked until barge-in is real; eye-tracking mode; web-agent hand-off; ghost/second cursor.

**Standing dissents:** Sally holds that mic-pause is an accessibility bug, accepted only under her contract. Winston holds that "complete operating system" is three products in a trench coat and the shell must stay a shell. Mary flags landing-page expectations as a churn risk for user zero ("correct in writing now").
